Trend Analysis

Forecasting next-day returns for four tickers, and finding the honest limit of what price history can predict.

This project is for educational purposes only. The forecasts produced by this model are not investment advice. Past performance does not predict future returns. The author does not recommend trading on these predictions.

The question

Can a model predict tomorrow better than a coin flip?

Given ten years of daily prices for four tickers, can we forecast the next day's log return better than a naive baseline? We did not assume the answer was yes.

4
tickers: an index plus three tech names
10y
daily history, multiple market regimes
3
stakeholders: a retail investor, an analyst, a reviewer

The data (Milestone 1)

Ten years, four tickers, one reproducible pipeline

  • Yahoo Finance, pinned date window, committed CSV snapshots and seed 42
  • We model log returns, not prices: an ADF test showed prices are non-stationary (p ≥ 0.86), returns are stationary (p = 0.0000)
  • Chronological split: train to 2023, validation 2024, holdout 2025 onward
  • Next-day target built per ticker so no label leaks across a split

What the data shows, part 1 (Milestone 2)

Volatility comes in clusters

Calm stretches and stormy stretches bunch together. The autocorrelation of squared returns is strong at lag one (about +0.49).

The size of tomorrow's move is predictable. That is the signal a GARCH variance model is built to capture.

What the data shows, part 2 (Milestone 2)

Direction is close to noise

Returns have almost no linear autocorrelation, and the best feature correlation with tomorrow is under 0.075. Mutual information says volatility features carry what little signal exists.

Variance is predictable. Direction is not. This is the efficient market showing up in the data before we fit a single model.

How we modeled it (Milestone 3)

Six models, simple to complex, each earning its place

baselines→ ARIMA→ GJR-GARCH→ LightGBM→ attention LSTM→ ensemble
  • ARIMA for the return mean, GJR-GARCH for the asymmetric variance the leverage effect implies
  • LightGBM on 39 features including VIX, yields and the dollar; an attention LSTM over 60-day windows
  • Leakage-free by design: tuning uses a purged walk-forward cross-validation, the holdout was opened exactly once

Error analysis (Milestone 4)

The model misses every shock, on purpose

The ten worst errors are all news: the DeepSeek shock, the April tariff crash and rebound, an earnings day. None of it is in past prices, so the model predicts near zero and misses.

The confusion matrix has an empty "predicted down" row. Its 0.542 accuracy is just the up-day rate. Error spikes in April 2025, the tariff month.

Limitations and ethics

Why the ceiling is real, and who could get hurt

  • Efficient markets: public price history is already priced in, so predictability is bounded
  • One calm regime: the 2025 holdout is lower volatility than training; no claim generalizes to a crisis
  • Survivorship bias: three current large-cap survivors, and the index is not directly tradeable
  • Multiple comparisons: six variants tested, so treat a single significant number with care
  • Misuse: a retail investor trading on this can lose real money; accuracy near 54% is below trading costs
  • Information asymmetry: a retail user competes against quant funds with far better data and speed
  • Disclaimer, always: not investment advice; past performance does not predict returns

Business impact, with a heavy disclaimer

A costless edge that costs erase

  • The promoted model is always long, so its backtest is just buy-and-hold: no timing value
  • A real timer (ARIMA, 174 switches) edges the market at zero cost
  • At 10 basis points per trade the edge is gone: $10,000 ends at $10,707 versus $12,666 held
A hindsight backtest on one calm regime is not a tradeable strategy. Not investment advice.

What I learned

The honest result was earned, not assumed

  • An off-by-one bug in ARIMA produced a fake edge that vanished once fixed
  • An un-tuned LightGBM overfit; a purged walk-forward CV shrank it to two trees and fixed it
  • Five proposed volatility features were all rejected by the ablation
  • The LSTM edge did not reproduce on the real GPU run
  • Every milestone was reviewed by adversarial subagents before it was called done

Questions

The takeaway, and what I expect you to ask

No model trained on price history beat a naive forecast out of sample. The one edge that survives is the market's drift, and it dies to costs. Measuring that ceiling carefully is the result.

Did it fail because nothing beat naive?
No. Measuring the efficient-market ceiling honestly is the finding, and it matches theory.
Why did the LSTM edge disappear?
It was run-dependent noise. The real GPU run is a coin flip, and reporting that is the honest choice.
Where could a future project add value?
Variance is predictable where direction is not, so volatility and risk forecasting, not return prediction.
