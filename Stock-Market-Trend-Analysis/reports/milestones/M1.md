## 1. Problem definition & business context

**Primary question:** Given 10 years of daily price data for the S&P 500 (`^GSPC`) and three large-cap tech stocks (`AAPL`, `AMZN`, `NVDA`), can we forecast next-day **log returns** better than naive baselines?

**Stakeholders:** retail investors, portfolio analysts, academic supervisors.

**Why time-series methods:** prices have drift, volatility clustering, and regime shifts (COVID 2020, 2022 rate hikes, 2023 AI boom). Random split would leak future into training.

**Course fit:** Weeks 4–6 (cleaning, feature engineering) → sets up Weeks 7–11 (EDA + ARIMA/LSTM in M3).

**Not investment advice.** Educational project only.

## 5. Outlier detection — rolling MAD (revised)

**Why MAD over IQR:** IQR uses fixed quantile thresholds over the whole history. Returns are fat-tailed and the variance changes by regime, so the IQR's "1.5 × IQR" fence flags too aggressively in calm periods and too leniently in volatile periods. Rolling MAD adapts to local volatility.

**Method:** for each ticker,
- `rolling_median_60` = 60-day rolling median of `log_return`
- `rolling_mad_60` = 60-day rolling MAD = `median(|log_return - rolling_median_60|)`
- `mad_z_score` = `(log_return − rolling_median_60) / rolling_mad_60`
- `is_outlier_mad` = `1` when `|mad_z_score| > 3.5`

| Ticker | `is_outlier_mad` (rolling MAD z>3.5) | Flag rate | `is_extreme_return` (`|r|>5%`, reference only) |
|---|---|---|---|
| `^GSPC` | 180 | 7.16% | 13 |
| `AAPL` | 156 | 6.21% | 45 |
| `AMZN` | 147 | 5.85% | 74 |
| `NVDA` | 148 | 5.89% | 232 |

**Decision: retain all rows, use the MAD flag as a feature.** A 5% NVDA move in 2017 is genuinely anomalous; a 5% NVDA move on a 2024 earnings day is barely above its local median. The flag respects that.

The fixed `|r| > 5%` threshold is kept as `is_extreme_return` for interpretable comparisons in M2 (e.g. counting headline-grabbing days), but is **not** the primary outlier definition for modelling.

### Before / after comparison

| Ticker | Old IQR + `|r|>5%` flag count | New rolling-MAD flag count | Old flag rate | New flag rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `^GSPC` | 175 + 13 = 188 | 180 | 7.5% | 7.2% |
| `AAPL` | 151 + 45 = 196 | 156 | 7.8% | 6.2% |
| `AMZN` | 137 + 74 = 211 | 147 | 8.4% | 5.9% |
| `NVDA` | 119 + 232 = 351 | 148 | 14.0% | 5.9% |

NVDA's flag rate dropped from 14% to 5.9% — the rolling MAD correctly recognises that NVDA's daily volatility regime is higher than the others, so the same return magnitude isn't flagged as often.

## 7. Target diagnostics — kurtosis (side-by-side) + ADF

### Kurtosis: `Close` vs `log_return`

These measure different things — reporting both makes it explicit.

| Ticker | `skew(Close)` | `kurt(Close)` | `skew(log_return)` | `kurt(log_return)` |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `^GSPC` | +0.622 | **-0.609** | -0.690 | **16.791** |
| `AAPL`  | +0.280 | **-1.147** | -0.072 | **6.784** |
| `AMZN`  | +0.210 | **-0.932** | -0.043 | **5.085** |
| `NVDA`  | +1.484 | **+0.783** | +0.067 | **6.562** |

**Reading:**
- **`kurt(Close)`** is *negative* for ^GSPC / AAPL / AMZN. A trending price series spends time at low values early and high values late, with little mass in the middle — that's a U-shaped / bimodal distribution with thinner tails than normal. **NVDA's kurt(Close) is positive** because the recent AI-cycle re-rating left a long high-price tail.
- **`kurt(log_return)`** is *strongly positive* for all four (5–17). Daily returns are fat-tailed (leptokurtic) — the standard finance result.
- **^GSPC has the highest log-return kurtosis (16.8)** despite being a 500-stock basket. This is the **COVID March 2020 effect**: ^GSPC had a string of >5% moves in a window where its prior standard deviation was tiny, so those days are ~10-sigma events under the index's normal vol — pushing kurtosis very high. Single stocks have higher prior vol, so even their extreme days are fewer standard deviations out → lower kurtosis.

User's intuition that "an index should have lower kurtosis than its components" relies on a constant-correlation independence assumption. In practice, **correlations spike to ~1 during crises**, so the index doesn't get the diversification benefit precisely when it would most help — and its low normal vol makes crisis days look extra extreme.

### Stationarity (ADF, mean) AND volatility clustering (Ljung-Box on r²)

ADF tests only the conditional **mean** (unit root). It says nothing about the **variance** — and financial returns have time-varying variance (volatility clustering). We now test both.

| Ticker | ADF `Adj Close` p | ADF `log_return` p | Ljung-Box(r²) lag-1 p | ACF(r²) lag-1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `^GSPC` | **0.9916** | **0.0000** | **0.0000** | **0.453** |
| `AAPL`  | **0.9789** | **0.0000** | **0.0000** | 0.279 |
| `AMZN`  | **0.8631** | **0.0000** | **0.0000** | 0.155 |
| `NVDA`  | **0.9989** | **0.0000** | **0.0000** | 0.129 |

**Reading:** prices non-stationary (p ≫ 0.05); log returns **stationary in mean** (p = 0.0000) → ARIMA can fit them with `d=0`. BUT Ljung-Box on squared returns is p ≈ 0 with positive ACF(r²) (0.45 for ^GSPC) → returns are **conditionally heteroskedastic** (volatility clusters). Plain ARIMA's constant-variance Gaussian errors are misspecified for the variance — this **motivates ARIMA+GARCH** (or at minimum variance-aware interval forecasts) in M3. Calling the series simply "stationary" (as v2 did) was an overclaim; it is stationary in mean only.

## 9. Limitations of M1

1. Daily granularity hides intraday signal.
2. Only 4 tickers, all survivors — survivorship bias.
3. No exogenous features (earnings, macro, options-implied vol, news).
4. `^GSPC` is not directly tradeable (use SPY for real strategies).
5. Yahoo prices can be retroactively updated; snapshot pins values at run date.
6. **New limitation surfaced by the audit:** the rolling-MAD window (60 days) is itself a hyper-parameter — too short and the flag becomes too aggressive; too long and it doesn't adapt to regime change fast enough. 60 trading days ≈ 3 months is a reasonable default but worth revisiting in M3 if outlier features hurt model performance.
