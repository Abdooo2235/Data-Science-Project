> skill. On the sealed holdout, ARIMA and LightGBM are directional coin flips (0.514 / 0.499). Two models keep

> statistically-real-but-uneconomic signal, no usable skill. This is the milestone's honest result.

## Enhancement pass — exogenous data + LightGBM + attention + ensemble (2026-06-17)

Four advanced patterns were added to push for predictive lift. **Result: they add real in-sample/val signal
but confirm the out-of-sample ceiling — the more powerful model (LightGBM + exogenous data) actually
*overfits* the holdout.** That is itself the most instructive finding.

**1. Exogenous features (9 new, the only break from price-history-only).** Pulled VIX (fear/sentiment +
regime proxy), 10y/3m Treasury yields, and the dollar index from Yahoo via the same snapshot pipeline; merged
by date; engineered leakage-safe features (`vix_level, vix_log_change, vix_z_60, is_high_vol_regime,
tnx_level, tnx_change, term_spread, term_spread_change, dxy_log_return`). All are market-priced daily values
known at close *t* (no CPI-style release-lag lookahead). MODEL_FEATURES: 30 → **39**.

**2. LightGBM (tabular gradient-boosted trees), fully local.** Trained on the 39 features + `ticker`
categorical.

| LightGBM | RMSE | DirAcc | DirAcc p | DM vs naive |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **val** | 0.0207 | **0.539** | **0.015 (sig)** | 0.149 (ns) |
| **holdout** | 0.02165 | 0.499 | 0.979 | **0.003 (sig WORSE)** |

**8 of LightGBM's top-15 feature importances are the new exogenous features** (`vix_level`, `dxy_log_return`,
`vix_z_60`, `term_spread`, `tnx_level`, …) — so the exogenous data is genuinely *informative in-sample*. But
on the holdout LightGBM is **significantly worse than naive** (DM p=0.003) and directionally a coin flip
(0.499). **The exogenous features + model complexity bought a val edge that did not generalize — textbook
overfitting, and a sharper restatement of the EMH ceiling than ARIMA alone.**

**3. Attention LSTM.** The Keras model was upgraded with a Bahdanau-style **additive attention** layer that
weights all 60 timesteps (focus on informative days, e.g. a vol spike) instead of using only the last step.
Verified by a 2-epoch CPU smoke-test (runs end-to-end); full training is the Colab job — the real attention
result is still **pending**.

**4. Ensemble.** A z-scored **directional blend** of LightGBM + LSTM predictions (`zblend`). Reported
DirAcc-only (a z-blend is off the return scale, so RMSE is n/a by design). With the smoke LSTM it is
illustrative plumbing (DirAcc 0.518); the real blend awaits the Colab LSTM.

**Honest verdict on the enhancements:** the exogenous data is real, leakage-free, and demonstrably used — but
it **did not produce out-of-sample lift**. ARIMA's holdout numbers are unchanged; LightGBM overfit. This
*strengthens* the project's EMH conclusion: even good alternative data + a strong nonlinear model cannot beat
naive on next-day direction out-of-sample. New artifacts: `models/lgbm_global{,_final}.txt`,
`lstm_attention_smoke.keras`, `M3_fig_lgb_importance.png`; `holdout_predictions.parquet` gains `y_pred_lgb`.
Self-audit: **19/19**.

## 3. ARIMA (per ticker, order by BIC)

Causal one-step (append-then-forecast). Order by **BIC** (parsimonious):

| Ticker | Order | Raw-resid LB(10) p |
|---|---|---|
| `^GSPC` | (1,0,0) | 0.000 |
| `AAPL` | (0,0,1) | 0.000 |
| `AMZN` | (1,0,0) | 0.124 |
| `NVDA` | (1,0,0) | 0.003 |

ARIMA val: **RMSE 0.02033, DirAcc 0.521 (p=0.195 — not significant)**. The raw-residual Ljung-Box p≈0 for
^GSPC/AAPL is **not** a wrong-order signal — it persists at every order because the leftover structure is
**volatility (ARCH)**, which the GARCH model removes (§4 shows standardised residuals *are* white). RMSE ties
naive (Diebold-Mariano p=0.361).

## 4. GJR-GARCH(1,1) Student-t — variance model, evaluated as a variance model

AR(1) mean + GJR-GARCH(1,1) (`o=1` asymmetry) + Student-t errors; out-of-sample via `last_obs`.

| Ticker | ν (t dof) | γ (leverage) | mean vol | QLIKE | MZ R² | std-resid LB p | std-resid² LB p |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `^GSPC` | 5.8 | **0.233** | 0.82% | −8.68 | 0.053 | 0.499 | 0.775 |
| `AAPL` | 5.6 | 0.127 | 1.53% | −7.48 | 0.001 | 0.294 | 0.962 |
| `AMZN` | 5.1 | 0.100 | 1.80% | −7.06 | 0.002 | 0.976 | 0.994 |
| `NVDA` | 5.2 | 0.114 | 3.12% | −5.85 | 0.028 | 0.518 | 0.997 |

**Takeaways:** **γ > 0 every ticker** confirms M2's leverage effect inside the model; **ν ≈ 5** confirms fat
tails. The **standardised-residual Ljung-Box p-values (all > 0.29) and squared (all > 0.77)** show the AR+GJR-
GARCH **jointly whitened the mean and removed the ARCH** — a clean fit (and the proper rebuttal to the raw-resid
LB≈0 in §3). The low MZ R² (forecast variance explains little of *squared-return* realized variance) reflects
how noisy a 1-day realized-variance proxy is, not a bad model. GARCH's mean-forecast row in §6 (DirAcc 0.555) is
shown for completeness; its real value is the variance/interval forecast, not point return.

## 5. Global attention-LSTM — now trained (20 epochs)

Keras LSTM (2-layer, 64-unit) with a **Bahdanau additive attention** layer over the 60-day window, on the
`MODEL_FEATURES` + ticker one-hot, `StandardScaler` train-only, `shuffle=False`, early stopping. A `FULL_TRAIN`
flag auto-enables the real 20-epoch run on a Colab GPU (2-epoch smoke on local CPU); see
[`reports/COLAB_TRAINING_GUIDE.md`](../COLAB_TRAINING_GUIDE.md).

**Real result (20-epoch, CPU-verified locally; Colab reproduces):**

| LSTM+Attention | RMSE | DirAcc | DirAcc p |
|---|---|---|---|
| **val** | 0.02083 | **0.539** | **0.036 (sig)** |
| **holdout** | 0.02086 | **0.542** | **0.005 (sig)** — but RMSE worse than naive (DM p=0.002) |

So the attention-LSTM behaves like GJR-GARCH: a **weak but statistically significant directional edge that
survives to holdout (54.2%, p=0.005)**, while its RMSE does not beat naive. The M2 mutual-information rationale
(vol features ~67× noise floor, invisible to Pearson) paid off *directionally* — but not economically (§8
backtest logic applies; a 54%-class edge dies to costs).

**Pooled holdout — all models vs naive (the only unbiased test):**

| Model | RMSE | vs naive (DM p) | DirAcc | DirAcc p |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| naive_zero | 0.02109 | — | n/a | — |
| ARIMA | 0.02107 | 0.783 (tie) | 0.514 | 0.294 (coin flip) |
| **GJR-GARCH-t (mean)** | 0.02106 | 0.589 (tie) | **0.543** | **0.002 (significant)** |
| LightGBM | 0.02165 | **0.003 (WORSE)** | 0.499 | 0.979 (coin flip) |

**Honest reading:** on **RMSE** no model beats naive (all DM p ≫ 0.05; LightGBM and the LSTM are significantly
*worse*). On **direction**, ARIMA and LightGBM are coin flips — but **two models keep a weak, statistically
significant OOS directional edge: GJR-GARCH-mean 54.3% (p=0.002) and the attention-LSTM 54.2% (p=0.005).**
Caveats that keep this honest: (1) it's economically marginal — both tie or trail naive on RMSE and the
§-below backtest shows a 54%-class directional edge is erased by transaction costs; (2) five models were tested
(multiple-comparison risk); (3) the edge almost certainly rides the same negative lag-1 mean-reversion
microstructure M2 flagged, not genuine alpha. So: **a whisker of statistically-real but uneconomic directional
signal (GARCH + LSTM); no usable out-of-sample skill overall.** Predictions: `data/processed/holdout_predictions.parquet`.

### Backtest (illustrative — NOT investment advice)

^GSPC, long next day when ARIMA > 0 else cash, **212 long-days, 175 position switches**:

| | terminal | ann ret | Sharpe | maxDD |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| buy & hold | ×1.267 | 17.3% | **0.99** | −18.9% |
| strategy 0 bps | ×1.274 | 17.7% | 1.18 | −10.3% |
| strategy 5 bps | ×1.167 | 11.3% | 0.75 | −10.5% |
| strategy 10 bps | ×1.070 | 4.9% | 0.33 | −12.7% |

At **zero cost** the strategy edges buy-and-hold on Sharpe (1.18 vs 0.99) — but it switches position **175
times**, so at a realistic **10 bps/switch it collapses to ×1.070 (Sharpe 0.33)**, far below passive. And the
strategy's daily returns are **not significantly positive** (t=1.38, p=0.168). Conclusion: no tradeable edge
once costs and significance are accounted for.

## Limitations / open items (for M4)

1. **LSTM not yet fully trained** — only a smoke-test locally; real number pending the Colab run. The
   "ARIMA+GARCH vs LSTM" comparison has only the classical arm so far.
2. **Selection bias on val** — order + best-model chosen on val; holdout (unbiased) shows no skill, which is the
   number to trust.
3. **GARCH variance** evaluated with a noisy squared-return realized-variance proxy; M4 could use a
   range-based (Parkinson/Garman-Klass) realized variance and a VaR/Kupiec exceedance backtest.
4. **Single holdout regime** (calm 2025–26) — one regime; conclusions can't claim generality across bull/bear.
5. **Multiple comparisons** across 4 tickers × orders × models — treat any single "win" skeptically.
